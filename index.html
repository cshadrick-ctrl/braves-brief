Then
A few thousand dollars was a story.
Now
A few thousand dollars is a Tuesday parlay.
Powerball trained the country to think in eight zeros. Crypto trained a generation to think in ten. Every week, a kid on TikTok screenshots a sportsbook payout that would have been life-changing money in 1995, and a fan in 2026 calls it lunch.
We didn't change. The fantasy did.

A M M U N I T I O N
The human insight
Nobody plays the lottery for the odds.
They play for the daydream.
08
A M M U N I T I O N
Powerball booms at $800M
and yawns at $40M.
Not because $40M wouldn't change a life. It would change almost any life in this country. But at $800M, the fantasy goes feral. The brain runs free. The mortgage is gone, the in-laws are taken care of, the boat is named, the speech is written.
At $40M, the brain stalls. The fantasy doesn't catch. The buying behaviour evaporates.
The daydream is the product. The dollar amount is just its container.

A M M U N I T I O N
And there's a second mechanic
People don't decide to play at the counter.
They decide on the way.
The Powerball player decides in the car. The sports bettor builds a parlay over coffee for a game that night. The daydream is fully formed long before money changes hands. The transaction is a closing motion, not a decision.

A M M U N I T I O N
Why the 50/50 underperforms
The Braves 50/50 fails on both counts.
i. The fantasy
The prize sits in the dead zone.
Too big to be a beer round. Too small to be a new life. There's nothing for the fan's brain to picture.
ii. The timing
The journey is four seconds long.
The customer journey has been compressed into a booth exchange during a game. Four seconds isn't enough time for a fantasy to form.

This isn't a tagline.

A M M U N I T I O N
What the work has to do
Five behavioural levers. All five, or none.
01
Move the decision upstream.
The 50/50 can't rely on a four-second booth moment to close a sale. The campaign does its emotional work hours before the fan arrives, so the decision is settled by first pitch.
02
Make the jackpot ambient.
The number is the most important asset in this product. Put it everywhere a Braves fan looks. Phone, podcast, feed, digital screens at The Battery, BravesVision ticker. Make it feel like the weather.
03
Build it social before transactional.
A fan who's told a friend they're playing has already bought. Create reasons to commit publicly. Group-chat-friendly content. "You in?" mechanics. Social pressure does the conversion, not the seller.
04
Make the stadium the payoff, not the pitch.
Every touchpoint from earlier in the day pays off in-park. BravesVision runs the climb. The activation around the winner is loud enough to recruit next week's buyers in real time.
05
Treat winners as the headline talent.
Real winners, real Atlanta, real lives changed. Their stories run constantly. They're the most credible proof the daydream is real - the thing that makes tomorrow's daydream real for the next fan.

A M M U N I T I O N
Tone
Confident. Vivid. A little loose.
A city that knows it's the South's biggest team and acts like it.
Not charitable. Not solemn. Not earnest in the careful way nonprofits often are. The 50/50 should feel like it belongs in the same conversation as a Powerball headline, a viral parlay, a kid stealing a base on national television.
The voice of the friend in the group chat who already knows the pot and is asking if you're in.

A M M U N I T I O N
Mandatories
  • Keep the Foundation cause visible for donors and stewardship. It's the benefit, not the headline.
  • Honor Harrah's Cherokee Casinos as the presenting partner. Don't design everything around them.
  • The creative lives everywhere a fan looks. Phone alerts. Radio. Social. Broadcast. BravesVision. In-park. The Battery. The app.
What we're not doing
  • Not leading with players or the cause. The dream—and the chance to win—moves people. (The Foundation benefits are a bonus, not the hook.)
  • Not using 'help the Foundation' to sell tickets. Yes, proceeds support great work. But that's not why fans play. They play for the jackpot and the feeling of possibility.
  • Not making the booth the campaign. The booth is the close. The dream and the desire happen before they walk through the gates.

A M M U N I T I O N
What success looks like
Seven cascading behaviours that prove the strategy works.
01
Unprompted checking
Fans open the app to track the pot without a push. Ambient presence and pull-based engagement are working.
02
Peer initiation
Friends bring it up first in group chats, not the brand. Viral and social proof mechanics are real.
03
Pre-arrival intent
Fans arrive with the decision already made, not considering it at the booth. Decision moved upstream as planned.
04
The winner becomes the moment
Fans stay to see the reveal, not as an afterthought. Payout is culturally significant.
05
Beyond-stadium engagement
Fans check the pot on non-game nights from other cities. Habit formation and geographic reach are real.
06
Frictionless activation
Booth interactions are instant, zero hesitation. Upstream work removed all barriers.
07
Cascading adoption
One player tells friends, who tell friends. Peer pressure and FOMO mechanics work.

A M M U N I T I O N
Case Study
Edmonton Oilers
$102M in sales
Provincial Geofencing
Not limited to stadium. Anyone in Alberta buys online. Buyer pool expands from 18K to millions.
Progressive Series Pots
Playoff draws span Games 1–4. Jackpot snowballs for a full week instead of resetting.
Aggressive Early-Bird Prizing
Buy before 2 PM? Win a truck. Before tipoff? Win a vacation. Eliminates hesitation.
Early-bird volume → inflated pot → FOMO for late buyers.
23
A M M U N I T I O N
Case Study
Buffalo Bills
$1M+ pots
Multi-Game Rollover
Pot rolls across an entire month of home games. One massive draw at the end, not resets after each game.
Survives Game Variables
A Tuesday night game against a weak opponent won't kill momentum. The pot carries forward.
Compounding Urgency
By the final game of the month, the jackpot is so massive that even casual fans feel mathematically compelled to participate.
One massive draw beats multiple small ones.
24
A M M U N I T I O N
Case Study
San Francisco 49ers
$115K–$175K per game
Omnipresent Kiosks
Permanent 50/50 stations at every major gate, plaza, and club level. Fans don't hunt for a vendor.
High-Volume Bundles
Offer tiered pricing (200 tickets for set price). Fans feel like they're holding massive "chances."
Normalized Purchase
Buying becomes part of the stadium experience, not an interruption.
Zero friction + perceived value = higher average spend.

A M M U N I T I O N
Case Study
LA Rams
$342K single game
Hyper-Specific Cause
Not "proceeds benefit the foundation." Instead: "funding first-generation college scholarships for underserved South LA students."
Premium Zone Targeting
Heavy kiosk placement in VIP suites and club levels. Higher-value buyers in one place.
Psychological Shift
Moves the transaction from "gambling" to "donating with a bonus chance to win."
Specificity drives conversion.
